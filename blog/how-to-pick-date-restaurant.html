How to Pick a Date Restaurant Without Sending 47 Texts

Here is the framework. Save it, print it, tattoo it on the inside of your eyelids. The reason most people fail at picking a date restaurant is they treat it like an optimization problem with infinite variables. It's not. It's a decision tree with five steps. Follow the steps. Stop texting your group chat at 4pm panicking.

Step 1: What's the stage?

The stage determines the energy level. This is non-negotiable and gets more wrong than any other variable.

  • First date: Low pressure plus easy exit. You need a place where "I should probably head out" at the 60-minute mark doesn't require an exit interview. Small plates, bar seats, casual bistros. Do NOT book a four-course tasting menu on a first date unless you enjoy the specific flavor of trapped regret.
  • Second date: Food-forward, some ambition. You're signaling that you listened on date one and are willing to invest. But still not a tasting menu.
  • Third or fourth date: Pick a neighborhood you both like and a place with more energy. This is when activity-style dinners work (communal tables, open kitchens, places where there's stuff to watch).
  • Anniversary or milestone: Now you can Michelin. Albi, Bresca, Rose's Luxury, Fiola Mare, Komi. This is where the splurge is earned.

Step 2: What's the budget?

Set a budget before you start browsing. Otherwise you'll drift upward by $15 per scroll until you're booking Pineapple and Pearls for a first date and wondering why you can't afford groceries next week.

Rough DC calibration:

  • Under $40 per person: All-Purpose, Bar Charley, Doi Moi happy hour, Ted's Bulletin, Compass Rose at the bar, Timber Pizza, Maketto.
  • $40-$80: Le Diplomate, Maydan, Unconventional Diner, Anju, Rose's Luxury, Tiger Fork, Barcelona Wine Bar, The Dabney.
  • $80-$150: Bresca, Fiola Mare, Little Pearl, Kinship, Imperfecto, The Dabney tasting, Blue Duck Tavern.
  • $150+: Pineapple and Pearls, Komi, Minibar, Jônt, Marcel's. Anniversary only.

Step 3: What's the exit plan?

A good first date has a built-in exit. Pick a place where "I should get going" doesn't create a scene. Small plates and bar seating are the easiest — you can leave after one drink or one round. Fixed tasting menus are the hardest. Once you start, you're locked in for two hours minimum. Calibrate based on how well you know the person.

Step 4: Have you asked about dietary restrictions?

Ask. Before booking. This is not a loyalty test, a personality assessment, or an imposition. It's basic human calibration. "Hey, any dietary stuff I should know before I pick a place?" takes three seconds and prevents an entire category of disasters. The absolute worst version of a first date is the one where your date reveals they're vegetarian halfway through the menu at a steakhouse.

Step 5: Book it and stop deliberating

The single worst thing you can do after selecting a restaurant is text five variations of "what do you think of this?" The best thing you can do is text "Booked us at Le Diplomate at 7:30 on Thursday — let me know if that doesn't work and I'll adjust." Decisiveness is attractive. Deliberation is not. I know this feels counterintuitive because you're trying to be considerate, but "considerate" in this case is indistinguishable from "incapable of making a choice," and one of those is attractive and the other one is very, very not.

The bonus step: the "small touch"

If you want to move from "good at picking restaurants" to "great at date planning," add one small touch. Call the restaurant and mention that it's a second date and you'd love a nice table — they'll often seat you better. Show up 5 minutes early and ask the host for a good booth. Order a bottle of wine instead of by-the-glass if you know they like that style. These are small signals that you're making an effort. They compound.

The decision tree, compressed

First date, $, Wednesday night, 7:30, small plates or bar seats, walkable from metro, book 2-3 days ahead. That's the default. Anything else requires adjustment to one of those variables based on actual context. Most of the time, the default works.
