9 DC Date Night Mistakes You're Definitely Making (And How to Fix Them)

I have, at this point, watched an absolutely spectacular number of DC dates go sideways. Sometimes for big reasons (personality mismatch, hidden ex trauma, one of them turned out to be a crypto guy). But most of the time it's small, preventable, structural errors. The same nine mistakes, over and over. Here they are, along with the fixes.

Mistake 1: Suggesting the Wharf on a Saturday night

It'll be slammed. Parking is a nightmare. You'll spend date one yelling over a cover band at a bar that charges $18 for a vodka soda. Go Tuesday, Wednesday, or early Sunday evening instead. Or go on a weekday for a walk during the day. The Wharf is genuinely great — just not on Saturday at 7pm.

Mistake 2: Booking at 9pm for a first date

First dates should end before exhaustion sets in. 7:00 or 7:30 is the sweet spot. You can always extend. You can never un-extend. A 9pm start time means you're both tired before you begin and if the date is going well, you're now committing to a 11:30pm finish, which is a lot of commitment for a first date.

Mistake 3: The surprise dress code

If the restaurant has a dress code, tell them. Nothing ends a date faster than watching your date walk in dressed for a casual bar and realize everyone around them is in blazers. "Nothing crazy, just the kind of place where a button-up works" is literally enough information. Takes 10 seconds. Saves the whole night.

Mistake 4: Picking a chain "to be safe"

Chains are not safe. Chains are boring. Boring is the worst first impression you can make. DC has 300+ great independent spots in the DatingDex directory — there is literally no excuse for taking someone to The Cheesecake Factory on a date in this city. Unless your date specifically asks for The Cheesecake Factory, in which case, well, that's a whole different set of signals to decode.

Mistake 5: Not making a reservation

"Let's just walk in" is a personality disorder on a first date. It works for brunch on a Tuesday. It does not work for dinner on a Friday in Logan Circle. Book something. Even if you cancel later. Walking in assumes everything will work out, which is a bad strategy in dating and also in life.

Mistake 6: Splitting the check weirdly

Just split it or offer to pay. Do not pull out the calculator app. Do not Venmo at the table. Do not have the "well, you had the extra cocktail" conversation. Nothing ends a date faster than a calculator on a dinner table. If you're worried about the money, pick a cheaper restaurant next time. The vibe penalty of the calculator is worth approximately fifteen restaurants.

Mistake 7: Ignoring what they said they like

If they mentioned on date one that they love wine, pick a wine bar for date two. If they said they're vegetarian, don't book a steakhouse. If they said they want to try more international cuisine, pick a Burmese or Salvadoran spot instead of another American bistro. Listening is free, effortless, and extraordinarily effective. The fact that most people don't do it is a bug in the market you can exploit.

Mistake 8: Trying to be the entertainment

This is a sneaky one. You do not need to be on. You do not need to have a funny story ready for every silence. You do not need to perform "fun person." The best dates I've had and seen are the ones where both people let there be small gaps in the conversation and trusted the room to do some of the work. Pick a good venue and let the venue do the entertainment.

Mistake 9: The "let's keep going" trap

If the date is going well, do NOT automatically default to "let's get another drink." Sometimes the best move at the end of a great first date is to end it while you both want more. Leaving at the peak is a power move. It creates what psychologists call the Zeigarnik effect — your brain holds onto unfinished experiences more tightly than finished ones. If you end the date while you're both having fun, your date will remember the night as incredible. If you extend it until one of you gets tired or a little bored, they'll remember the drop-off, not the peak.

The meta-lesson

Most dating advice focuses on what to say or how to be. But most dates don't fail because of what you said. They fail because of structural errors in logistics, timing, and venue choice. Fix those and the "what to say" problem solves itself, because you'll both be in a good venue at a good time with enough space to actually have a conversation.
